# Endocrine and Nutrition Reference

Covers thyroid function, iron studies, and the major vitamins relevant to
routine outpatient screening: Vitamin D, Vitamin B12, Folate, and
Homocysteine. These tests have particular significance in the Indian
population, where deficiencies in Vitamin D, B12, and iron are very common.

## Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH)

TSH is produced by the pituitary gland and signals the thyroid to make
thyroid hormones. It is the most sensitive single screening test for
thyroid disease because it changes in the opposite direction of thyroid
hormone levels through a feedback loop.

**Reference range:** 0.4–4.0 mIU/L (some labs use slightly different
upper limits; the upper end rises with age).

**Low values can indicate (suppressed TSH):** Hyperthyroidism — Graves'
disease, toxic nodular goiter, thyroiditis (early phase), or excess thyroid
hormone replacement. Also seen in pituitary failure (a rarer cause).

**High values can indicate (elevated TSH):** Hypothyroidism — primary
thyroid failure from Hashimoto's thyroiditis, post-radiation, post-surgical,
iodine deficiency, or untreated/under-treated hypothyroidism. Mild
elevation (4–10 mIU/L) with normal Free T4 is "subclinical hypothyroidism".

**Important context:** Subclinical hypothyroidism is common, especially in
Indian women. The decision to treat depends on TSH level, symptoms, antibody
status, and clinical judgement. TSH alone cannot distinguish primary from
central thyroid problems — if TSH is normal but the patient has clear
symptoms, free T4 should be checked.

## Free T4 (Free Thyroxine)

Free T4 measures the unbound, biologically active form of the main thyroid
hormone (T4). Free T4 gives a more reliable picture than total T4 because
total T4 is affected by binding proteins.

**Reference range:** 0.8–1.8 ng/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Hypothyroidism (typically with elevated TSH);
central hypothyroidism (with low or inappropriately normal TSH).

**High values can indicate:** Hyperthyroidism; excess thyroid hormone
replacement; certain thyroiditis phases.

**Important context:** TSH + Free T4 together define the four main patterns:
high TSH + low Free T4 = overt hypothyroidism; high TSH + normal Free T4 =
subclinical hypothyroidism; low TSH + high Free T4 = overt hyperthyroidism;
low TSH + normal Free T4 = subclinical hyperthyroidism.

## Free T3 (Free Triiodothyronine)

Free T3 measures the unbound form of the more biologically active thyroid
hormone. T3 is produced both by the thyroid and by conversion from T4 in
peripheral tissues.

**Reference range:** 2.3–4.2 pg/mL.

**Low values can indicate:** Severe non-thyroidal illness ("sick euthyroid
syndrome", where T3 falls as a non-specific response to illness),
hypothyroidism, or starvation.

**High values can indicate:** Hyperthyroidism, especially T3-toxicosis (a
form of hyperthyroidism with disproportionate T3 elevation, more common in
some Indian patients with toxic nodular goiter).

**Important context:** T3 is not routinely needed for diagnosing
hypothyroidism (TSH and Free T4 are sufficient) but is useful in evaluating
hyperthyroidism.

## Total T3 and Total T4

Total T3 and Total T4 measure all circulating thyroid hormone, both bound
(to thyroxine-binding globulin) and free.

**Reference ranges:** Total T3 80–200 ng/dL; Total T4 5.1–14.1 μg/dL.

**Important context:** Total T3 and T4 are affected by changes in
binding-protein levels (pregnancy, oral contraceptives, liver disease,
nephrotic syndrome can all alter these proteins). Free forms are preferred
when available.

## Anti-Thyroid Peroxidase (Anti-TPO) Antibodies

Anti-TPO antibodies are markers of autoimmune thyroid disease.

**Reference range:** Less than 35 IU/mL is typically negative; positive
above ~35–60 IU/mL (varies by assay).

**High values can indicate:** Hashimoto's thyroiditis (the most common
cause of hypothyroidism worldwide), Graves' disease, postpartum thyroiditis,
or other autoimmune thyroid conditions.

**Important context:** Anti-TPO positivity is common in subclinical
hypothyroidism and identifies people more likely to progress to overt
hypothyroidism. Many people with positive Anti-TPO have normal thyroid
function for years.

## Serum Iron

Serum iron measures the iron currently circulating in blood, bound to
transferrin.

**Reference range:** Men 65–175 μg/dL; Women 50–170 μg/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Iron deficiency, anemia of chronic disease,
inflammation, or recent menstruation (in women).

**High values can indicate:** Iron overload conditions such as hereditary
hemochromatosis, repeated blood transfusions, chronic liver disease, or
recent iron supplementation.

**Important context:** Serum iron fluctuates throughout the day (highest in
morning) and is heavily affected by recent meals or iron supplements. Alone,
it is not a reliable indicator of total body iron stores — ferritin is far
more useful for that purpose.

## Ferritin

Ferritin is a protein that stores iron inside cells. The level in blood
correlates with the body's iron stores, making it the single best blood
test to assess iron status.

**Reference range:** Men 24–336 ng/mL; Women 11–307 ng/mL (postmenopausal
women have ranges closer to men's).

**Low values can indicate:** Depleted iron stores (iron deficiency).
Ferritin below 30 ng/mL is highly specific for iron deficiency, and below
15 ng/mL is essentially diagnostic. Low ferritin is often the *first*
laboratory sign of iron deficiency, appearing before hemoglobin falls.

**High values can indicate:** Iron overload (hemochromatosis, repeated
transfusions); but more commonly an inflammatory or stress response, since
ferritin is also an acute-phase reactant. Liver disease, chronic infections,
autoimmune diseases, malignancy, and severe COVID can all raise ferritin
independent of iron stores.

**Important context:** A normal or high ferritin in the presence of
inflammation does NOT rule out iron deficiency. In such cases, transferrin
saturation and other iron studies help. In the Indian population, iron
deficiency is extremely common, particularly among women of reproductive
age and adolescents.

## Total Iron-Binding Capacity (TIBC)

TIBC measures the blood's total capacity to bind iron — essentially a
measure of transferrin levels.

**Reference range:** 240–450 μg/dL.

**High values can indicate:** Iron deficiency (the body upregulates
transferrin to capture available iron), pregnancy, or estrogen therapy.

**Low values can indicate:** Anemia of chronic disease, malnutrition,
nephrotic syndrome, or iron overload.

**Important context:** High TIBC with low ferritin and low transferrin
saturation = classic iron deficiency. Low TIBC with normal or high ferritin
and low transferrin saturation = anemia of chronic disease.

## Transferrin Saturation (TSAT)

The percentage of transferrin actually carrying iron. Calculated as
(serum iron / TIBC) × 100.

**Reference range:** 20–50%.

**Low values can indicate:** Iron deficiency, anemia of chronic disease.

**High values can indicate:** Iron overload (hemochromatosis is suggested
by TSAT > 45% in women or > 50% in men), excess iron supplementation, or
hemolytic anemia.

## Vitamin D (25-Hydroxyvitamin D)

The 25-OH form of vitamin D is the standard measure of vitamin D status,
reflecting both dietary intake and skin synthesis from sunlight.

**Reference categories:** Sufficient ≥ 30 ng/mL; Insufficient 20–29 ng/mL;
Deficient < 20 ng/mL; Severely deficient < 10 ng/mL.

**Low values can indicate (vitamin D deficiency):** Limited sun exposure
(common in indoor lifestyles, urban living, conservative dress), darker
skin pigmentation (reduces UVB-driven synthesis), low dietary intake (few
foods contain meaningful amounts), malabsorption (celiac disease, IBD,
post-bariatric surgery), liver or kidney disease (impaired activation),
obesity (sequestered in fat tissue), or older age.

**High values:** Excess supplementation (greater than ~150 ng/mL can cause
toxicity with hypercalcemia).

**Important context:** Vitamin D deficiency is extremely common in Indian
adults — some studies report 70–90% prevalence — despite India's sunny
climate, because of indoor lifestyles, skin pigmentation, air pollution
filtering UVB, and conservative clothing. Vitamin D deficiency is
associated with bone disease (osteomalacia, increased fracture risk),
muscle weakness, and a range of other systemic effects.

## Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)

Vitamin B12 is essential for red-cell production, DNA synthesis, and
nervous system function. It is found almost exclusively in animal foods.

**Reference range:** 200–900 pg/mL (some labs use 211–911).

**Low values can indicate (B12 deficiency):** Inadequate intake (especially
in vegetarian or vegan diets — very common in India), impaired absorption
(pernicious anemia from autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells,
atrophic gastritis, ileal disease, H. pylori infection, post-bariatric
surgery), medications (metformin with long-term use, proton pump
inhibitors), or rare genetic causes.

**High values:** Usually not clinically significant; may reflect
supplementation or, rarely, certain liver diseases or myeloproliferative
disorders.

**Important context:** B12 deficiency causes macrocytic anemia (high MCV)
and can cause irreversible neurological damage if prolonged. Indian
vegetarians are at very high risk and many specialists recommend routine
screening. Serum B12 between 200 and 350 pg/mL may still represent functional
deficiency; methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine can clarify borderline
cases.

## Folate (Folic Acid)

Folate is a B vitamin essential for DNA synthesis and red-cell formation.
Unlike B12, it is found in many plant foods.

**Reference range:** 2.7–17.0 ng/mL (serum folate); red-cell folate is
preferred for chronic status when available.

**Low values can indicate:** Inadequate dietary intake, malabsorption (celiac
disease, IBD), increased requirement (pregnancy, hemolysis), alcohol use,
or certain medications (methotrexate, phenytoin).

**High values:** Generally not clinically significant; often reflects
supplementation.

**Important context:** Folate deficiency causes macrocytic anemia very
similar to B12 deficiency. Importantly, folate supplementation can correct
the anemia of B12 deficiency while leaving the neurological damage
unaddressed — so B12 should always be checked alongside folate before
starting folate-only supplementation.

## Homocysteine

Homocysteine is an amino acid that accumulates when B12, folate, or B6
metabolism is impaired. It is a sensitive marker of functional B12/folate
status and an independent cardiovascular risk factor.

**Reference range:** 5–15 μmol/L.

**High values can indicate:** B12 deficiency (most common cause),
folate deficiency, B6 deficiency, kidney disease, hypothyroidism, certain
medications, or genetic variants in folate metabolism (e.g., MTHFR
polymorphisms).

**Important context:** Elevated homocysteine in the presence of a
"borderline" B12 level (200–350 pg/mL) supports a diagnosis of functional
B12 deficiency. Hyperhomocysteinemia is independently associated with
cardiovascular disease, stroke, and cognitive decline, though direct
treatment to lower homocysteine has shown mixed clinical benefit.
