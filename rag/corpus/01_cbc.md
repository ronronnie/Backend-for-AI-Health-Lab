# Complete Blood Count (CBC) Reference

The Complete Blood Count measures the cellular components of blood: red
cells, white cells, and platelets, along with red-cell indices that describe
their size and hemoglobin content. It is the single most commonly ordered
blood test and is used to screen for anemia, infection, bleeding disorders,
and many systemic diseases.

## Hemoglobin (Hb)

Hemoglobin is the iron-containing protein in red blood cells that carries
oxygen from the lungs to body tissues. The hemoglobin concentration is the
single most important value in a CBC for diagnosing and grading anemia.

**Indian adult reference range:** Men 13.5–17.5 g/dL; Women 12.0–15.5 g/dL.
In pregnancy the lower limit is typically 11.0 g/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Anemia from many possible causes — iron
deficiency (the most common cause worldwide), vitamin B12 or folate
deficiency, anemia of chronic disease, chronic kidney disease, recent blood
loss, hemolysis (red-cell destruction), or inherited disorders such as
thalassemia or sickle cell disease.

**High values can indicate:** Polycythemia (an excess of red cells), which
may be primary (polycythemia vera, a bone-marrow disorder) or secondary to
dehydration, smoking, chronic lung or heart disease, sleep apnea, or living
at high altitude.

**Important context:** Hemoglobin alone does not tell you the *cause* of
anemia; always interpret alongside MCV (red-cell size) and red-cell indices,
and follow up with ferritin and other iron studies, B12/folate, or further
tests as indicated. Severity grading: mild (10 g/dL to lower normal limit),
moderate (7.0–9.9), severe (below 7.0 g/dL).

**Critical thresholds:** Hemoglobin below 7.0 g/dL is severe anemia and
often warrants urgent clinical evaluation, especially if the person is
symptomatic (severe fatigue, breathlessness, chest pain).

## Red Blood Cell (RBC) Count

The RBC count is the number of red blood cells per unit volume of blood.
It complements hemoglobin and hematocrit in characterizing red cell mass.

**Indian adult reference range:** Men 4.5–5.9 million/μL; Women 4.0–5.2 million/μL.

**Low values can indicate:** Anemia of any cause — bleeding, hemolysis,
nutritional deficiencies (iron, B12, folate), bone-marrow disorders, chronic
disease, or kidney disease (low erythropoietin).

**High values can indicate:** Erythrocytosis or polycythemia, from similar
causes as elevated hemoglobin — dehydration, smoking, chronic hypoxia, or
primary marrow disorders. Thalassemia trait can produce a paradoxically
high or normal RBC count alongside low hemoglobin and low MCV, which is one
of the classic clues to distinguish it from iron deficiency.

**Important context:** RBC count, hemoglobin, and hematocrit usually move
together. Discordant patterns (e.g. low Hb with high RBC) raise the
possibility of thalassemia trait.

## Hematocrit (HCT) / Packed Cell Volume (PCV)

Hematocrit is the percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells.
It is essentially a different way of measuring the same thing as hemoglobin
and the RBC count.

**Indian adult reference range:** Men 41–53%; Women 36–46%.

**Low values can indicate:** Anemia of any cause.

**High values can indicate:** Polycythemia, dehydration, chronic hypoxia.

**Important context:** A rough rule of thumb is that hematocrit is about 3×
the hemoglobin value. A markedly elevated hematocrit (above ~55%) increases
the risk of thrombosis. In dehydration, all three measurements (Hb, RBC, HCT)
rise together because plasma volume contracts.

## Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV)

MCV is the average size of a red blood cell. It is the single most useful
index for classifying anemia.

**Indian adult reference range:** 80–100 fL (femtolitres).

**Low values can indicate:** Microcytic anemia. The two major causes are
iron deficiency and thalassemia. Less common causes include anemia of
chronic disease (sometimes microcytic), lead poisoning, and certain other
inherited disorders.

**High values can indicate:** Macrocytic anemia. The classic causes are
vitamin B12 deficiency and folate deficiency. Other causes include
hypothyroidism, liver disease, alcohol use, certain medications (methotrexate,
hydroxyurea), and reticulocytosis (when the bone marrow produces many young,
slightly larger red cells in response to hemolysis or blood loss).

**Important context:** Always interpret MCV alongside hemoglobin. A low MCV
with normal hemoglobin still suggests an underlying iron or hematologic
issue. The combination low MCV + low MCH + low ferritin makes iron
deficiency far more likely than thalassemia.

## Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin (MCH)

MCH is the average amount of hemoglobin per red blood cell.

**Indian adult reference range:** 27–32 pg.

**Low values can indicate:** Hypochromic anemia — red cells with less
hemoglobin than normal, typically due to iron deficiency or thalassemia.

**High values can indicate:** Macrocytic anemia (larger cells naturally
contain more hemoglobin). B12 and folate deficiency are the most common
causes.

**Important context:** MCH typically moves in parallel with MCV. The
combination of low MCV with low MCH is the signature of microcytic
hypochromic anemia (most often iron deficiency).

## Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration (MCHC)

MCHC is the average concentration of hemoglobin within each red cell.

**Indian adult reference range:** 32–36 g/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Iron deficiency anemia (red cells become pale
and less hemoglobin-dense).

**High values can indicate:** Hereditary spherocytosis is the classic cause
of an unusually high MCHC; otherwise mildly raised MCHC may simply reflect
laboratory factors.

**Important context:** MCHC has a narrow normal range and is less
specifically useful than MCV or MCH in classifying anemia.

## Red Cell Distribution Width (RDW)

RDW measures the variation in size of red blood cells. A high RDW means red
cells of widely different sizes are present (anisocytosis).

**Indian adult reference range:** 11.5–14.5%.

**High values can indicate:** Many causes of anemia, including iron
deficiency (often the first index to rise in early iron deficiency), mixed
nutritional deficiency (iron + B12), recent blood transfusion, or recovery
from anemia. RDW is normal in pure thalassemia trait — a useful
discriminator from iron deficiency, where RDW is usually elevated.

**Important context:** A high RDW with low MCV strongly suggests iron
deficiency rather than thalassemia, where RDW is typically normal.

## White Blood Cell (WBC) / Total Leukocyte Count (TLC)

WBC count is the total number of white blood cells per unit volume of blood.
It reflects immune system activity and is used to detect infection,
inflammation, and blood disorders.

**Indian adult reference range:** 4,000–11,000/μL.

**Low values can indicate (leukopenia):** Viral infections, bone-marrow
suppression (from drugs, chemotherapy, radiation, or certain illnesses),
nutritional deficiencies (B12, folate), autoimmune destruction, hypersplenism,
or hematologic malignancies.

**High values can indicate (leukocytosis):** Bacterial infection,
inflammation, stress response, corticosteroid use, smoking, hematologic
malignancies (leukemia), or recovery from a recent infection.

**Critical thresholds:** WBC below 1,000/μL (especially absolute neutrophils
below 500) is severe neutropenia with significant infection risk. WBC above
30,000/μL warrants careful evaluation for hematologic malignancy.

## Neutrophils

Neutrophils are the most abundant white cell type and the first responders
to bacterial infection.

**Indian adult reference range:** 40–75% of WBC; absolute count typically
2,000–7,500/μL.

**Low values can indicate (neutropenia):** Viral infection, chemotherapy,
bone-marrow disorders, autoimmune destruction, severe nutritional deficiency.

**High values can indicate (neutrophilia):** Bacterial infection, acute
inflammation, tissue injury (burns, trauma, surgery), stress, corticosteroid
therapy, smoking, certain leukemias.

**Important context:** Absolute neutrophil count (ANC) below 500/μL is
severe neutropenia and carries a high risk of serious infection.

## Lymphocytes

Lymphocytes (B cells, T cells, NK cells) drive adaptive immune responses
and respond particularly to viral infections.

**Indian adult reference range:** 20–45% of WBC; absolute count typically
1,000–4,800/μL.

**Low values can indicate:** Viral illnesses (including HIV), corticosteroid
use, recent severe stress, lymphoma, bone-marrow disorders, or severe
malnutrition.

**High values can indicate:** Viral infections (mononucleosis, hepatitis,
acute viral illness), pertussis, chronic infections such as tuberculosis,
or lymphoid malignancies (chronic lymphocytic leukemia).

## Eosinophils

Eosinophils are white cells involved in allergic responses and parasitic
infections.

**Indian adult reference range:** 1–6% of WBC.

**High values can indicate (eosinophilia):** Allergic conditions (asthma,
allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis, drug allergy), parasitic infections
(especially in tropical settings), some skin diseases, and rarer
hypereosinophilic syndromes or eosinophilic leukemia. In India, parasitic
causes are common and should be considered.

**Low values:** Are usually not clinically significant.

## Monocytes

Monocytes are the precursors of tissue macrophages and play roles in
chronic inflammation and phagocytosis.

**Indian adult reference range:** 2–10% of WBC.

**High values can indicate (monocytosis):** Chronic infections (especially
tuberculosis, brucellosis, endocarditis), autoimmune disease, certain
leukemias (especially chronic myelomonocytic leukemia), or recovery from
neutropenia.

## Platelet Count

Platelets are cell fragments essential for blood clotting.

**Indian adult reference range:** 150,000–450,000/μL.

**Low values can indicate (thrombocytopenia):** Decreased production
(bone-marrow disorders, vitamin deficiencies, viral infection, alcohol),
increased destruction (immune thrombocytopenia, drug-induced, thrombotic
microangiopathy), sequestration (splenomegaly), or dilution (massive
transfusion). In India, dengue, malaria, and other tropical viral fevers
are very common causes of acute thrombocytopenia.

**High values can indicate (thrombocytosis):** Reactive (infection,
inflammation, iron deficiency, post-splenectomy, malignancy) or primary
(essential thrombocythemia, other myeloproliferative disorders).

**Critical thresholds:** Platelets below 20,000/μL carry significant
spontaneous bleeding risk. Above 1,000,000/μL warrants evaluation for a
myeloproliferative disorder.

## Reticulocyte Count

Reticulocytes are immature red blood cells released from the bone marrow.
The reticulocyte count reflects how actively the marrow is producing red
cells.

**Reference range:** 0.5–2.5% of red cells (or absolute count 25,000–75,000/μL).

**Low values can indicate:** Inadequate marrow response to anemia — iron,
B12, or folate deficiency that has not been corrected; aplastic anemia;
chronic kidney disease (low erythropoietin); marrow suppression.

**High values can indicate:** Active marrow response — recent blood loss,
hemolysis, or response to treatment of nutritional deficiency.

**Important context:** A patient with anemia and a low reticulocyte count
has a production problem; a patient with anemia and a high reticulocyte
count has a destruction or loss problem. This single distinction guides
much of the workup of anemia.
