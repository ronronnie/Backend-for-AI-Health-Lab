# Metabolic Panel Reference

Covers lipid profile, glucose and HbA1c, liver function tests (LFT), and
kidney function tests (KFT). These panels together evaluate cardiovascular
risk, diabetes status, and the function of the two largest metabolic organs
(liver and kidneys).

## Total Cholesterol

Total cholesterol is the sum of all cholesterol carried in the blood,
including LDL, HDL, and VLDL fractions. It is a screening measure for
cardiovascular risk.

**Reference categories:** Below 200 mg/dL desirable; 200–239 borderline
high; 240 and above high.

**Low values:** Rarely a clinical concern in adults; very low levels can
sometimes accompany severe illness, malnutrition, hyperthyroidism, or liver
failure.

**High values can indicate:** Dyslipidemia, often associated with genetic
factors, diet, sedentary lifestyle, obesity, diabetes, hypothyroidism,
nephrotic syndrome, or certain medications.

**Important context:** Total cholesterol alone is a crude measure;
cardiovascular risk depends much more on the LDL and HDL components and on
overall risk factors. A high total cholesterol with very high HDL may
actually be protective, while a normal total cholesterol with low HDL can
still confer risk.

## LDL Cholesterol

Low-Density Lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol carries cholesterol from the liver
to the body's tissues and is the primary form deposited in arterial walls,
making it a key driver of atherosclerosis.

**Reference categories:** Below 100 mg/dL optimal; 100–129 near-optimal;
130–159 borderline high; 160–189 high; 190 and above very high.

**High values can indicate:** Cardiovascular risk. Causes include genetic
predisposition (familial hypercholesterolemia), high-saturated-fat diet,
sedentary lifestyle, obesity, diabetes, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome,
or chronic kidney disease. Target levels are lower for people with existing
heart disease or diabetes (often below 70 mg/dL).

**Low values:** Low LDL is generally considered favorable for cardiovascular
risk.

**Important context:** LDL is sometimes calculated rather than directly
measured. In people with very high triglycerides (above 400 mg/dL),
calculated LDL is inaccurate and a direct LDL measurement is preferred.

## HDL Cholesterol

High-Density Lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol carries cholesterol away from
the arteries back to the liver for disposal and is generally considered
"protective" against cardiovascular disease.

**Reference categories:** Men above 40 mg/dL; Women above 50 mg/dL.
Levels above 60 mg/dL are considered protective.

**Low values can indicate (low HDL):** Increased cardiovascular risk.
Common contributors include metabolic syndrome, obesity, sedentary
lifestyle, smoking, type 2 diabetes, and certain genetic patterns.

**High values:** Generally favorable, though very high HDL (above 100 mg/dL)
in some genetic conditions may not confer additional benefit.

**Important context:** Low HDL is one of the strongest cardiovascular risk
markers in South Asian populations, where it is very common.

## Triglycerides

Triglycerides are fats stored in adipose tissue and circulated in the blood
between meals. They are a key marker of metabolic health.

**Reference categories:** Below 150 mg/dL normal; 150–199 borderline high;
200–499 high; 500 and above very high.

**Low values:** Rarely clinically significant.

**High values can indicate:** Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome,
prediabetes or diabetes, obesity, excess alcohol, hypothyroidism,
nephrotic syndrome, certain medications, or genetic disorders. Very high
levels (above 500–1000 mg/dL) increase the risk of pancreatitis.

**Important context:** Triglycerides require a fasting sample for accurate
interpretation (12-hour fast). High triglycerides + low HDL together is the
"atherogenic dyslipidemia" pattern of metabolic syndrome.

## VLDL Cholesterol

Very Low-Density Lipoprotein (VLDL) carries triglycerides from the liver to
tissues. It is usually calculated as triglycerides ÷ 5.

**Reference range:** 5–40 mg/dL.

**High values:** Track with high triglycerides and reflect the same
underlying metabolic issues.

**Important context:** VLDL is rarely measured directly and adds little
beyond LDL, HDL, and triglycerides.

## Fasting Plasma Glucose

Glucose measured after at least 8 hours of fasting. The standard test for
screening and diagnosing diabetes.

**Reference categories:** 70–99 mg/dL normal; 100–125 prediabetes (impaired
fasting glucose); 126 and above on two separate occasions confirms diabetes.

**Low values can indicate (hypoglycemia):** Below 70 mg/dL is hypoglycemia.
Causes include diabetes medications (especially insulin and sulfonylureas),
prolonged fasting, severe illness, alcohol, certain hormone deficiencies,
or rare insulin-producing tumors.

**High values can indicate (hyperglycemia):** Diabetes mellitus,
prediabetes, stress hyperglycemia (during acute illness), Cushing's syndrome,
acromegaly, pancreatic disease, or certain medications (corticosteroids,
some antipsychotics).

**Critical thresholds:** Glucose below 50 mg/dL or above 400 mg/dL warrants
urgent attention.

## Postprandial (2-hour) Plasma Glucose

Glucose measured 2 hours after a standardized 75g glucose load (oral glucose
tolerance test) or after a typical meal.

**Reference categories:** Below 140 mg/dL normal; 140–199 prediabetes
(impaired glucose tolerance); 200 and above confirms diabetes.

**Important context:** The 2-hour value detects diabetes earlier than
fasting glucose in some people, particularly those with predominantly
post-meal glucose spikes. Common in Indian populations.

## HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)

HbA1c reflects the average blood glucose concentration over the previous
2–3 months by measuring the percentage of hemoglobin that has glucose
attached to it.

**Reference categories:** Below 5.7% normal; 5.7–6.4% prediabetes; 6.5% or
above on two occasions confirms diabetes.

**High values can indicate:** Diabetes or prediabetes, poor glycemic control
in known diabetes.

**Low values:** Below normal can occur in conditions that shorten red-cell
lifespan (hemolysis, recent transfusion, anemia) or in certain
hemoglobinopathies, falsely lowering the result.

**Important context:** HbA1c can be unreliable in: any condition that
changes red-cell turnover (iron deficiency anemia can falsely *raise* HbA1c;
recent treatment of iron deficiency can falsely lower it), pregnancy, recent
blood loss or transfusion, and hemoglobinopathies common in some Indian
populations. Always interpret alongside fasting and postprandial glucose
when possible.

## Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT / SGPT)

ALT is an enzyme found mainly in the liver. It is released into the blood
when liver cells are injured. It is the most sensitive routine marker of
hepatocellular (liver-cell) damage.

**Reference range:** 7–56 U/L (slightly higher in men than women).

**High values can indicate:** Hepatitis (viral, alcoholic, drug-induced,
autoimmune), fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MAFLD — extremely common in Indian
adults), medication side effects (statins, antibiotics, paracetamol
toxicity, anti-tuberculosis drugs), or muscle injury (less commonly).

**Important context:** Mildly elevated ALT in an otherwise well person is
most commonly due to non-alcoholic fatty liver disease in the Indian
context. Levels above 5× the upper limit warrant urgent evaluation;
above 10× suggests acute hepatitis.

## Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST / SGOT)

AST is an enzyme present in liver, heart, and skeletal muscle. It rises in
liver injury but is less liver-specific than ALT.

**Reference range:** 10–40 U/L.

**High values can indicate:** Liver injury, muscle injury (after intense
exercise, rhabdomyolysis), heart attack (historically a cardiac marker;
troponin is now preferred), or hemolysis.

**Important context:** The AST/ALT ratio is a useful pattern. AST > ALT
(ratio above 1.5–2.0) classically suggests alcoholic liver disease or
advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis. ALT > AST is the typical pattern in NAFLD and
most other liver conditions.

## Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP)

ALP is an enzyme found in liver, bone, intestine, and placenta. Elevated
ALP can come from any of these sources.

**Reference range:** 44–147 U/L in adults (substantially higher in children
during growth, and in pregnancy due to placental origin).

**High values can indicate:** Cholestatic liver disease (bile-flow
obstruction — stones, tumors, primary biliary disorders), liver
infiltration, bone disease (Paget's, osteomalacia, healing fractures,
metastases), pregnancy, or growth in children.

**Important context:** A simultaneously high GGT confirms the source as
liver. A high ALP with normal GGT suggests a bone origin.

## Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase (GGT)

GGT is an enzyme present in the liver and biliary system. It is sensitive
to liver damage and to cholestatic processes.

**Reference range:** Men 8–61 U/L; Women 5–36 U/L.

**High values can indicate:** Cholestatic liver disease (with elevated ALP),
alcohol use, fatty liver disease, certain medications (anticonvulsants),
or any biliary obstruction.

**Important context:** GGT is the most sensitive marker of alcohol use among
routine liver tests. Persistently elevated GGT in the absence of obvious
liver disease should prompt evaluation for alcohol use or chronic
medication effects.

## Total Bilirubin

Bilirubin is the breakdown product of hemoglobin. Total bilirubin includes
both unconjugated (indirect) and conjugated (direct) forms.

**Reference range:** 0.1–1.2 mg/dL.

**High values can indicate (hyperbilirubinemia, the cause of jaundice):**
Hemolysis (excess production), liver disease (impaired processing),
biliary obstruction (impaired excretion), or genetic disorders such as
Gilbert syndrome (a common, benign cause of mildly elevated indirect
bilirubin).

**Important context:** Jaundice typically becomes clinically visible when
total bilirubin exceeds about 2.5–3.0 mg/dL. The pattern of conjugated vs.
unconjugated elevation distinguishes among causes.

## Direct (Conjugated) Bilirubin

Direct bilirubin is the form that has been processed by the liver and is
ready for excretion in bile.

**Reference range:** 0.0–0.3 mg/dL.

**High values can indicate:** Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis) or
biliary obstruction (gallstones, tumors, strictures). Conjugated
hyperbilirubinemia is always pathological — never a benign finding.

## Indirect (Unconjugated) Bilirubin

Indirect bilirubin is the form before liver processing.

**Reference range:** 0.2–0.8 mg/dL.

**High values can indicate:** Hemolysis (excess production from rapid
red-cell breakdown), Gilbert syndrome (a common benign genetic variant
affecting ~5–10% of people), or neonatal jaundice.

## Total Protein

Total protein is the sum of all serum proteins, mainly albumin and
globulins.

**Reference range:** 6.0–8.3 g/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Malnutrition, liver disease (decreased
production), kidney disease (loss in urine), severe burns, inflammatory
disease.

**High values can indicate:** Dehydration (relative increase), chronic
inflammatory conditions, multiple myeloma or other paraproteinemias.

## Albumin

Albumin is the most abundant blood protein, produced exclusively by the
liver. It maintains oncotic pressure and transports many substances in
blood.

**Reference range:** 3.5–5.0 g/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Chronic liver disease (impaired synthesis),
nephrotic syndrome (urinary loss), malnutrition, chronic inflammation
(albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant), or severe burns.

**High values:** Usually due to dehydration only.

**Important context:** Albumin has a long half-life (~20 days), so it
reflects chronic rather than acute changes. Low albumin in a hospitalized
patient is a marker of severity of illness.

## A/G Ratio (Albumin/Globulin Ratio)

The ratio of albumin to globulins (calculated as albumin / (total protein
− albumin)).

**Reference range:** 1.0–2.5.

**Low values can indicate:** Either low albumin (chronic disease, liver
disease, nephrotic syndrome) or high globulin (chronic inflammation,
chronic infection, multiple myeloma).

**Important context:** A reversed A/G ratio is a useful flag for chronic
inflammation, immunoglobulin disorders, or chronic liver disease.

## Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) / Blood Urea

Urea is the main nitrogenous waste product of protein metabolism, excreted
by the kidneys.

**Reference range:** Urea 15–40 mg/dL; BUN 7–20 mg/dL (BUN = urea ÷ 2.14).

**Low values can indicate:** Malnutrition, severe liver disease, overhydration.

**High values can indicate:** Reduced kidney function, dehydration, high-protein
diet, gastrointestinal bleeding, heart failure, or use of certain drugs.

**Important context:** Urea is influenced by many factors besides kidney
function (hydration, diet, GI bleeding). Creatinine and eGFR are more
specific measures of kidney function.

## Creatinine

Creatinine is a waste product of muscle metabolism, filtered by the kidneys
at a relatively constant rate. It is the standard marker for estimating
kidney function.

**Reference range:** Men 0.7–1.3 mg/dL; Women 0.6–1.1 mg/dL.

**Low values:** Usually reflect low muscle mass (elderly, severe malnutrition,
some chronic illnesses) and rarely indicate kidney disease.

**High values can indicate:** Reduced kidney function (acute or chronic),
dehydration, urinary tract obstruction, or — less commonly — high muscle
mass or recent intense exercise.

**Important context:** Creatinine varies with muscle mass, sex, age, and
diet. A "normal" creatinine in an elderly low-muscle-mass person can mask
significant kidney dysfunction. Always interpret with eGFR.

## eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)

eGFR is a calculation that estimates how well the kidneys are filtering
blood, derived from creatinine, age, sex, and sometimes race.

**Reference categories:** 90 or above normal; 60–89 mildly reduced; 30–59
moderately reduced (CKD Stage 3); 15–29 severely reduced (Stage 4); below
15 kidney failure (Stage 5).

**Important context:** Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as an eGFR
below 60 mL/min/1.73m² sustained for 3 months or more, or evidence of
kidney damage (e.g., proteinuria) regardless of eGFR. eGFR may be
inaccurate at the extremes of body size, in pregnancy, in people with very
low or high muscle mass, and during acute kidney injury.

## Uric Acid

Uric acid is the end product of purine metabolism. It is excreted by the
kidneys.

**Reference range:** Men 3.4–7.0 mg/dL; Women 2.4–6.0 mg/dL.

**High values can indicate (hyperuricemia):** A high-purine diet (red meat,
seafood, alcohol especially beer), reduced renal excretion, metabolic
syndrome, certain drugs (diuretics, low-dose aspirin), chronic kidney
disease, tumor lysis after chemotherapy, or genetic disorders.
Hyperuricemia is the underlying cause of gout, though many people with
hyperuricemia never develop gouty attacks.

**Low values:** Generally have little clinical significance.

**Important context:** Hyperuricemia is increasingly recognized as a
component of metabolic syndrome and is associated with hypertension,
fatty liver, and cardiovascular risk.
