# Clinical Patterns in Lab Reports

Individual lab values gain meaning when grouped into recognized clinical
patterns. This section describes named patterns that combinations of values
fit, with the defining features of each. Patterns are descriptive, not
diagnostic — they suggest where to look next, not what the patient has.

## Microcytic Anemia Pattern

**Defining features:** Low hemoglobin + low MCV (below 80 fL) + low MCH
(below 27 pg). RDW is often elevated.

**Most common causes:** Iron deficiency anemia is by far the most common,
worldwide and especially in India where it remains the leading cause of
anemia in women and adolescents. Thalassemia trait is the next major
consideration, particularly relevant in Indian populations where carrier
rates can reach 4–5%.

**Distinguishing iron deficiency from thalassemia trait:** Iron deficiency
typically has low ferritin, low serum iron, low transferrin saturation,
high TIBC, and high RDW. Thalassemia trait usually shows normal or high
ferritin, normal or high RBC count (paradoxically high despite low Hb),
normal RDW, and an abnormal hemoglobin electrophoresis. The "Mentzer index"
(MCV ÷ RBC count) under 13 favors thalassemia; over 13 favors iron
deficiency.

**Other causes:** Anemia of chronic disease (sometimes microcytic), lead
poisoning, and rarer disorders.

## Macrocytic Anemia Pattern

**Defining features:** Low hemoglobin + high MCV (above 100 fL).

**Most common causes:** Vitamin B12 deficiency and folate deficiency.
Other causes include hypothyroidism, liver disease, alcohol use, certain
medications (methotrexate, hydroxyurea, anticonvulsants), reticulocytosis
from hemolysis, myelodysplastic syndrome, and pregnancy.

**Important context:** In the Indian context — particularly with the high
prevalence of vegetarian diets — B12 deficiency is a leading cause of
macrocytic anemia. Folate deficiency is now less common since flour
fortification programs but can still occur. B12 and folate should both be
checked before initiating supplementation, because folate alone can
correct the anemia of B12 deficiency while letting the neurological damage
progress unaddressed.

## Normocytic Anemia Pattern

**Defining features:** Low hemoglobin with normal MCV.

**Most common causes:** Anemia of chronic disease (chronic infection,
autoimmune disease, malignancy, chronic kidney disease), acute blood loss,
hemolysis, early iron deficiency, mixed nutritional deficiencies, and
bone-marrow disorders.

**Approach:** The reticulocyte count helps distinguish a production problem
(low retics — chronic disease, marrow failure) from a destruction or loss
problem (high retics — bleeding, hemolysis).

## Iron Deficiency Anemia

**Defining features:** Microcytic hypochromic anemia + low ferritin (most
specific, especially below 30 ng/mL) + low serum iron + low transferrin
saturation + high TIBC + high RDW.

**Important context:** The most common nutritional deficiency worldwide
and the leading cause of anemia in Indian women of reproductive age.
Causes include inadequate intake, blood loss (menstrual, gastrointestinal,
or other), increased demand (pregnancy, lactation, growth), and impaired
absorption (celiac disease, atrophic gastritis, H. pylori infection,
post-bariatric surgery). In men or postmenopausal women, occult
gastrointestinal blood loss must be considered until proven otherwise.

## Anemia of Chronic Disease

**Defining features:** Normocytic or mildly microcytic anemia + normal or
*high* ferritin + low serum iron + low transferrin saturation + low or
normal TIBC.

**Important context:** Common in chronic infections, autoimmune diseases,
chronic kidney disease, and malignancy. Ferritin is high because it is an
acute-phase reactant; iron is sequestered in storage and unavailable for
red-cell production. This pattern can coexist with true iron deficiency,
complicating interpretation.

## Vitamin D Deficiency

**Defining features:** Serum 25-OH vitamin D below 20 ng/mL.

**Indian context:** Vitamin D deficiency is extremely prevalent in Indian
adults — many published studies suggest 70–90% of urban Indians have
insufficient or deficient levels. Drivers include limited daylight sun
exposure (indoor lifestyles, air pollution filtering UVB), darker skin
pigmentation reducing efficiency of UVB-driven synthesis, conservative
clothing in some populations, and the near-absence of dietary vitamin D in
typical Indian diets (very few foods contain meaningful amounts; staples
are not fortified).

**Associated findings:** Low or low-normal calcium, low phosphorus,
elevated parathyroid hormone (PTH), elevated alkaline phosphatase in
established deficiency, and bone pain or proximal muscle weakness in
severe cases. Severe long-standing deficiency causes osteomalacia in
adults and rickets in children.

## Vitamin B12 Deficiency

**Defining features:** Serum B12 below 200 pg/mL (clearly low) or 200–350
pg/mL with elevated methylmalonic acid or homocysteine (borderline with
functional confirmation).

**Indian context:** Very common among Indian vegetarians, vegans, and the
elderly. Pernicious anemia (autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal
cells) is a less common but important cause that can occur in any diet.

**Associated findings:** Macrocytic anemia (high MCV, often with
hypersegmented neutrophils on smear), elevated homocysteine, elevated
methylmalonic acid, and in advanced cases neurological symptoms (peripheral
neuropathy, gait disturbance, cognitive changes) that may be irreversible
if not treated.

## Subclinical Hypothyroidism Pattern

**Defining features:** TSH mildly elevated (typically 4–10 mIU/L) with
Free T3 and Free T4 in the normal range.

**Indian context:** Very common, especially in women. Hashimoto's
thyroiditis is the most frequent underlying cause; Anti-TPO antibodies
help establish autoimmunity.

**Important context:** A meaningful fraction of cases progress to overt
hypothyroidism over years, and the rate is higher in those with positive
Anti-TPO antibodies. Treatment decisions depend on the TSH level, symptoms,
pregnancy plans, antibody status, and lipid levels.

## Overt Hypothyroidism Pattern

**Defining features:** Elevated TSH (typically above 10 mIU/L) with low
Free T4.

**Common causes:** Hashimoto's thyroiditis (most common), post-radioiodine
or post-thyroidectomy, severe iodine deficiency (rare in India after
iodization), congenital hypothyroidism, or certain medications (amiodarone,
lithium).

## Hyperthyroidism Pattern

**Defining features:** Suppressed TSH (below 0.4 mIU/L) with elevated Free
T3 and/or Free T4.

**Common causes:** Graves' disease (autoimmune, most common in younger
adults), toxic multinodular goiter or toxic adenoma (more common in older
adults), thyroiditis (transient hyperthyroidism followed by hypothyroidism),
or rarely excessive thyroid hormone replacement.

**Variants:** "Subclinical hyperthyroidism" is suppressed TSH with normal
free hormones. "T3-toxicosis" is hyperthyroidism with disproportionate T3
elevation, more common in older Indian patients with nodular thyroid
disease.

## Fatty Liver / NAFLD Pattern

**Defining features:** Mildly elevated ALT and AST (usually ALT > AST) with
normal or only mildly elevated bilirubin, normal albumin, normal coagulation.
ALP and GGT may be normal or mildly elevated.

**Indian context:** Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD, more recently
called MAFLD — metabolic-associated fatty liver disease) is now the most
common chronic liver disease worldwide, including in India. It strongly
co-occurs with metabolic syndrome features: central obesity, prediabetes
or diabetes, dyslipidemia, and hypertension.

**Important context:** A fatty liver pattern of liver enzymes in someone
with metabolic risk factors usually does not need an extensive workup
unless enzymes are markedly elevated, the pattern doesn't fit, or there
are signs of advanced disease. Ultrasound and FibroScan are common
non-invasive next steps.

## Cholestatic Liver Pattern

**Defining features:** Elevated ALP + elevated GGT, with or without
elevated bilirubin. ALT and AST may be normal or mildly elevated.

**Causes:** Bile duct obstruction (gallstones, strictures, tumors),
intrahepatic cholestasis (primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing
cholangitis, drug-induced cholestasis, infiltrative liver diseases), or
certain medications (anabolic steroids, oral contraceptives in some
people).

**Important context:** Elevated ALP without GGT elevation suggests a bone
rather than liver source. The combination of elevated ALP + GGT confirms
the liver/biliary system as the source.

## Hepatocellular Injury Pattern

**Defining features:** Markedly elevated ALT and/or AST (often more than
3–5 times the upper limit), with ALP and GGT relatively less elevated.

**Causes:** Acute viral hepatitis (A, B, C, E — Hepatitis E is endemic in
India), drug-induced liver injury (paracetamol overdose is a leading cause,
also anti-tuberculosis drugs, antibiotics, herbal supplements), alcoholic
hepatitis (AST > ALT pattern), autoimmune hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis,
or rare metabolic disorders.

**AST/ALT ratio significance:** AST > ALT, especially a ratio above
1.5–2.0, classically suggests alcoholic liver disease or advanced
fibrosis/cirrhosis. ALT > AST is more typical of acute viral hepatitis,
drug injury, and NAFLD.

## Prediabetes Pattern

**Defining features:** Any one of: fasting glucose 100–125 mg/dL,
postprandial 2-hour glucose 140–199 mg/dL, or HbA1c 5.7–6.4%.

**Indian context:** South Asians develop type 2 diabetes at lower BMI
levels than Western populations ("thin-fat" phenotype) and have a high
rate of progression from prediabetes to diabetes. India's diabetes
prevalence is among the highest globally.

**Important context:** Prediabetes substantially increases cardiovascular
risk even before diabetes develops and identifies a high-risk window
where lifestyle interventions (where appropriate, under clinical
guidance) can delay or prevent progression.

## Type 2 Diabetes Pattern

**Defining features:** Any one of (confirmed on a second occasion):
fasting glucose ≥ 126 mg/dL, HbA1c ≥ 6.5%, 2-hour OGTT glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL,
or random glucose ≥ 200 mg/dL with classic symptoms.

**Indian context:** Type 2 diabetes is extremely prevalent in India and
typically presents at younger ages and lower BMIs than in Western
populations. Associated findings often include hypertension, dyslipidemia
(low HDL, high triglycerides), elevated liver enzymes (NAFLD), elevated
uric acid, and central obesity.

## Dyslipidemia Patterns

**General dyslipidemia:** Any of LDL ≥ 130, total cholesterol ≥ 200,
triglycerides ≥ 150, or HDL below sex-specific cutoffs.

**Atherogenic dyslipidemia (metabolic syndrome pattern):** Elevated
triglycerides + low HDL together — a strong marker of insulin resistance
and metabolic syndrome. Highly prevalent in South Asian populations.

**Familial hypercholesterolemia pattern:** LDL ≥ 190 mg/dL (in adults) or
the combination of very high LDL with a strong family history of premature
cardiovascular disease. Heritable, requires earlier and more aggressive
clinical management.

## Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Pattern

**Defining features:** eGFR below 60 mL/min/1.73m² sustained for 3 months
or more, OR markers of kidney damage (proteinuria, hematuria, structural
abnormalities) regardless of eGFR.

**Common associated findings:** Elevated creatinine, elevated BUN, anemia
(reduced erythropoietin production by kidneys), elevated phosphorus, low
calcium with elevated PTH (chronic kidney disease–mineral bone disorder),
metabolic acidosis (low bicarbonate), and elevated potassium in advanced
stages.

**CKD staging:** Stage 1 (eGFR ≥ 90 with kidney damage), Stage 2 (60–89
with damage), Stage 3a (45–59), Stage 3b (30–44), Stage 4 (15–29), Stage 5
(< 15 or on dialysis).

**Indian context:** Diabetic kidney disease and hypertensive kidney
disease together account for the majority of CKD in India. Many cases are
detected late.

## Hyperuricemia and Gout Pattern

**Defining features:** Uric acid above 7.0 mg/dL (men) or 6.0 mg/dL (women).
In gout flares, joint inflammation and elevated CRP/ESR also occur.

**Associated findings:** Hyperuricemia is part of the metabolic syndrome
constellation — often co-occurs with hypertension, fatty liver, insulin
resistance, and obesity. Many people with hyperuricemia never develop
clinical gout; conversely, gout can occur at "normal" uric acid levels
during an acute flare.

## Metabolic Syndrome (Lab Markers)

**Lab features that suggest metabolic syndrome:** Multiple of: elevated
fasting glucose (≥100 mg/dL), low HDL (men <40, women <50), elevated
triglycerides (≥150 mg/dL), elevated uric acid, and elevated ALT/AST
suggesting fatty liver.

**Important context:** Full clinical diagnosis of metabolic syndrome also
requires waist circumference and blood-pressure measurements, which are
not on lab reports. Lab findings alone can strongly *suggest* the
constellation. South Asian Indians have higher rates of metabolic syndrome
at lower BMI levels than other populations, and lower waist-circumference
cutoffs are used (≥90 cm in men, ≥80 cm in women).
