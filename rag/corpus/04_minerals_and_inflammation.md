# Minerals, Electrolytes, and Inflammation Markers Reference

Covers the major electrolytes and minerals on a routine chemistry panel,
plus general markers of inflammation (CRP, ESR) and basic cardiac markers
that may appear on extended panels.

## Sodium (Na)

Sodium is the main extracellular cation and the principal determinant of
serum osmolality and water balance.

**Reference range:** 135–145 mEq/L.

**Low values can indicate (hyponatremia):** Water excess (heart failure,
liver cirrhosis, kidney disease, SIADH — syndrome of inappropriate ADH),
sodium loss (diarrhea, vomiting, diuretics), or excessive water intake.
Hyponatremia is one of the most common electrolyte abnormalities in
hospitalized patients.

**High values can indicate (hypernatremia):** Dehydration (most common —
inadequate water intake, diarrhea, vomiting, excess sweating), diabetes
insipidus, or rarely excess salt intake.

**Critical thresholds:** Sodium below 125 mEq/L or above 155 mEq/L can
cause neurological symptoms (confusion, seizures, coma) and warrants urgent
evaluation.

**Important context:** The rate of change matters as much as the absolute
number. Rapid changes in either direction are dangerous; chronic stable
abnormalities are better tolerated.

## Potassium (K)

Potassium is the main intracellular cation and is critical for cardiac
rhythm and neuromuscular function.

**Reference range:** 3.5–5.0 mEq/L.

**Low values can indicate (hypokalemia):** Diuretic use (especially loop
and thiazide diuretics), vomiting, diarrhea, alkalosis, primary
hyperaldosteronism, magnesium deficiency, or excessive licorice consumption.

**High values can indicate (hyperkalemia):** Kidney failure (most common
cause), ACE inhibitor or ARB therapy, potassium-sparing diuretics, severe
tissue injury (rhabdomyolysis, burns, tumor lysis), Addison's disease, or
hemolysis of the blood sample (a common spurious cause).

**Critical thresholds:** Potassium below 2.5 or above 6.0 mEq/L can cause
life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias and is a medical emergency. Values
above 6.5 require immediate evaluation.

**Important context:** A surprisingly high potassium in an otherwise well
person is often spurious from sample hemolysis — a repeat draw before
acting is appropriate when the clinical picture doesn't fit.

## Chloride (Cl)

Chloride is the main extracellular anion, usually moving with sodium.

**Reference range:** 98–107 mEq/L.

**Low values can indicate:** Vomiting, certain types of diuretic use,
respiratory acidosis with bicarbonate retention.

**High values can indicate:** Dehydration, certain types of metabolic
acidosis, excessive normal saline administration.

**Important context:** Chloride is rarely meaningful in isolation; it is
interpreted alongside sodium and bicarbonate to evaluate acid-base status
and the anion gap.

## Bicarbonate (HCO3 / CO2)

Bicarbonate (often reported as "CO2" or "total CO2" on chemistry panels)
reflects the body's main acid-base buffer.

**Reference range:** 22–29 mEq/L.

**Low values can indicate (metabolic acidosis):** Diabetic ketoacidosis,
lactic acidosis (sepsis, shock, ischemia), kidney failure, severe diarrhea,
or various poisonings.

**High values can indicate (metabolic alkalosis):** Vomiting, diuretic use,
or compensatory response to chronic respiratory acidosis (chronic lung
disease).

**Important context:** Bicarbonate combined with sodium and chloride gives
the anion gap, a key calculation in evaluating acidosis.

## Calcium (Total)

Total serum calcium includes ionized (biologically active) calcium and
protein-bound calcium (mainly to albumin).

**Reference range:** 8.5–10.5 mg/dL.

**Low values can indicate (hypocalcemia):** Vitamin D deficiency,
hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroid surgery is a classic cause), chronic
kidney disease, malabsorption, severe hypomagnesemia, or low albumin
(which lowers total but not ionized calcium).

**High values can indicate (hypercalcemia):** Primary hyperparathyroidism
(most common cause in outpatients — usually mild), malignancy (most common
cause in hospitalized patients), excess vitamin D or calcium supplementation,
or rarer causes (sarcoidosis, certain medications).

**Important context:** Calcium should be corrected for serum albumin
(add 0.8 mg/dL of calcium for every 1.0 g/dL decrease in albumin below 4.0).
Hypercalcemia with high or inappropriately-normal parathyroid hormone (PTH)
suggests primary hyperparathyroidism.

## Phosphorus

Phosphorus is essential for energy metabolism, bone, and many cellular
processes.

**Reference range:** 2.5–4.5 mg/dL.

**Low values can indicate:** Vitamin D deficiency, hyperparathyroidism,
malabsorption, refeeding syndrome, alcoholism, certain medications.

**High values can indicate:** Chronic kidney disease (a major cause —
phosphorus retention contributes to bone disease in CKD), tumor lysis
syndrome, hypoparathyroidism, or excessive intake.

**Important context:** Phosphorus, calcium, and parathyroid hormone are
tightly interlinked. Abnormalities in one usually accompany abnormalities
in the others.

## Magnesium

Magnesium is a cofactor for many enzyme systems and is critical for cardiac
and neuromuscular function.

**Reference range:** 1.7–2.4 mg/dL.

**Low values can indicate (hypomagnesemia):** Diuretic use, alcohol abuse,
proton pump inhibitor therapy (especially long-term), malabsorption,
chronic diarrhea, type 2 diabetes. Magnesium deficiency commonly accompanies
hypokalemia and hypocalcemia and is often missed.

**High values can indicate:** Kidney failure, excess intake (laxatives,
antacids containing magnesium).

**Important context:** Persistent hypokalemia and hypocalcemia that don't
correct with replacement often have underlying hypomagnesemia. Checking and
correcting magnesium is important in resistant electrolyte abnormalities.

## C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and High-Sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP)

CRP is an acute-phase protein made by the liver in response to inflammation.
Standard CRP detects clinically significant inflammation; hs-CRP detects
much lower levels and is used in cardiovascular risk assessment.

**Reference ranges:**
- Standard CRP: Less than 5 mg/L
- hs-CRP cardiovascular risk: <1.0 mg/L low risk; 1.0–3.0 mg/L average risk;
  >3.0 mg/L higher risk; >10 mg/L often suggests acute illness rather than
  chronic cardiovascular inflammation

**High values can indicate:** Bacterial infection, autoimmune disease
flares, tissue injury (post-surgery, trauma), inflammatory bowel disease,
chronic infections, malignancy, or — in lower-level elevations — chronic
low-grade inflammation associated with obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular
disease.

**Important context:** CRP rises within 4–6 hours of an inflammatory
stimulus and falls quickly with resolution, making it a useful marker for
tracking response to treatment. CRP is non-specific — it tells you
inflammation is present but not its cause.

## Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)

ESR measures how quickly red blood cells settle in a tube of blood — they
settle faster when inflammatory proteins increase blood viscosity.

**Reference range:** Men less than 15 mm/hr; Women less than 20 mm/hr.
Age-adjusted upper limits rise with age (rough rule: age divided by 2 for
men; age plus 10 divided by 2 for women).

**High values can indicate:** Inflammation, infection, autoimmune disease,
anemia, kidney disease, malignancy (especially multiple myeloma, where very
high ESR values are classic), or pregnancy.

**Important context:** ESR rises and falls more slowly than CRP, making it
better for tracking chronic conditions. ESR can also be elevated by
non-inflammatory causes such as anemia and age, so it is less specific
than CRP. Very high ESR (above 100 mm/hr) has a narrower differential —
multiple myeloma, temporal arteritis, severe infection, or metastatic
cancer being prominent considerations.

## Troponin (cardiac)

Troponin is a protein released into the blood when heart muscle cells are
damaged. It is the standard biomarker for diagnosing a heart attack.

**Reference range:** Less than the 99th-percentile cutoff specific to each
assay (typically less than 0.04 ng/mL for older assays; high-sensitivity
assays use much lower cutoffs measured in ng/L).

**High values can indicate:** Heart attack (most important consideration),
myocarditis, heart failure, severe hypertension, pulmonary embolism, kidney
failure (chronically elevated due to reduced clearance), severe sepsis, or
intense prolonged exercise.

**Important context:** Diagnosis of heart attack typically requires a
rising and/or falling pattern of troponin (serial measurements 1–6 hours
apart) combined with symptoms or ECG changes — not just a single elevated
value. Many non-cardiac conditions can elevate troponin and need to be
distinguished from acute myocardial infarction.

## NT-proBNP / BNP

NT-proBNP (and its precursor BNP) are released by the heart in response to
ventricular stretch. They are markers of heart failure.

**Reference range:** Highly age-dependent. Rough cutoffs for heart failure:
NT-proBNP > 125 pg/mL in younger adults, > 450 in those over 50, > 900
above 75. BNP > 100 pg/mL suggests heart failure.

**High values can indicate:** Heart failure (most important), atrial
fibrillation, pulmonary embolism, severe pulmonary hypertension, kidney
failure (impaired clearance raises levels), advanced age, or severe sepsis.

**Important context:** Values rise with age and kidney dysfunction; obesity
*lowers* values. A normal NT-proBNP makes heart failure very unlikely in
someone with breathlessness.
